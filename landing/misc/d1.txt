## DESIRED TAKEAWAY
Use AI Switchboard to turn multi-model debate into an auditable, human-owned decision record—then execute confidently in your existing CLI.

## PRESENTATION

- ### Slide 1 — The Real Problem Isn’t “Getting an Answer”
  - Answers are cheap; decisions are expensive  
  - You need reasoning you can revisit  
  - You need dissent, not averaged prose  
  - You need memory across weeks  
  **Image description:** A lone builder at a workbench facing three translucent “advisor” silhouettes, with a visible paper trail on the table.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - I’m not here to help you get answers faster.  
  - I’m here to help you decide with confidence.  
  - Because the cost isn’t the prompt.  
  - The cost is living with the consequences.

<hr>

- ### Slide 2 — Single-Model Chat Has Two Failure Modes
  - Disagreement gets smoothed into “one voice”  
  - Reasons disappear after the window closes  
  - You can’t audit how you got there  
  - Future-you re-litigates old choices  
  **Image description:** A chat bubble dissolving into fog, while a forked path sign fades behind it.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - Most tools optimize for fluent output.  
  - But real work needs visible tradeoffs.  
  - And it needs durable memory.  
  - Otherwise you repeat decisions endlessly.

<hr>

- ### Slide 3 — AI Switchboard: A Deliberation Council
  - Multiple models, structured deliberation rounds  
  - Preserved dissent, surfaced verbatim  
  - Human authority remains final  
  - Auditable decision memory persists  
  **Image description:** A “council table” diagram with labeled seats (Codex, Gemini, Claude), arrows between them, and a stamped decision record.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - Switchboard turns models into a council.  
  - Not a single oracle.  
  - You ask once; they deliberate together.  
  - You receive a structured verdict plus dissent.

<hr>

- ### Slide 4 — What It Deliberately Is Not
  - Not autonomous code execution  
  - Not “agents editing in parallel”  
  - Deliberation layer, not execution layer  
  - Your CLI remains the doer  
  **Image description:** Two-layer diagram: “Deliberation” above “Execution,” with a hard boundary line and a human hand on the boundary.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - This is a conscious design refusal.  
  - Deliberation must see a stable reality.  
  - Execution changes reality.  
  - Mixing them breaks trust and traceability.

<hr>

- ### Slide 5 — Local-First, Single-User, No Remote Surface
  - FastAPI bound to 127.0.0.1:8787  
  - SQLite state on your machine  
  - No auth because no public binding  
  - Exports happen only when you choose  
  **Image description:** A laptop with a loopback arrow “127.0.0.1” and a locked filing cabinet labeled “switchboard.db.”  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - Switchboard is local-only by default.  
  - It’s built for one person: you.  
  - Your orchestration state stays on your machine.  
  - Nothing leaves unless you export it.

<hr>

- ### Slide 6 — The Audit Philosophy: Nothing Gets “Summarized Away”
  - Every prompt and response stored verbatim  
  - Every task records source agent provenance  
  - Runs track tokens, timing, errors, costs  
  - Decisions are permanent task artifacts  
  **Image description:** An “audit log” timeline with entries: prompt, response, run metadata, decision stamp.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - The system treats reasoning as an artifact.  
  - Not a transient conversation.  
  - You can inspect every turn later.  
  - And you can see who submitted what.

<hr>

- ### Slide 7 — The Three Modes: Pick Your Shape
  - Conclave: equals deliberate to convergence  
  - Resolve: primary iterates with critiques  
  - Consult: propose, critique, finalize  
  - Choose mode by decision type  
  **Image description:** Three cards labeled Conclave, Resolve, Consult with distinct flow arrows.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - “More models” isn’t the point.  
  - The point is a deliberate structure.  
  - Different questions need different shapes.  
  - Switchboard makes that explicit.

<hr>

- ### Slide 8 — Conclave Mode: Independent Minds, Visible Dissent
  - N equal seats, full-mesh visibility  
  - Convergence signals: done, thinking, need input  
  - Unanimous by default, not forced  
  - Disagreements surfaced, not averaged  
  **Image description:** A roundtable with equal chairs; two chairs have “dissent” flags, all visible.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - Use conclave for defensible forks in the road.  
  - Architecture, refactors, security calls.  
  - If disagreement persists, you see it clearly.  
  - The orchestrator never hides it.

<hr>

- ### Slide 9 — Resolve Mode: One Driver, Others As Brakes
  - Primary chooses status each round  
  - Consultants critique until primary resolves  
  - Backstops bound time, rounds, repetition  
  - Ideal for debugging and deep dives  
  **Image description:** A lead car labeled “Primary” with two following cars labeled “Critique,” approaching a finish flag.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - Resolve is for “let’s get to ground truth.”  
  - One agent drives forward.  
  - Others keep it honest.  
  - And cost is bounded by backstops.

<hr>

- ### Slide 10 — Consult Mode: The Fast Second Opinion
  - Fixed three steps, always terminates  
  - Primary proposes, consultant critiques  
  - Primary finalizes with critique incorporated  
  - Perfect for “did I miss something?”  
  **Image description:** A three-step checklist: Draft → Critique → Final, with a timer icon.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - Consult is your speed tool.  
  - It’s not a debate tournament.  
  - It’s a critique pass with closure.  
  - Great for small reviews and sanity checks.

<hr>

- ### Slide 11 — The Keeper Model: Authority Stays Human
  - You are named “the keeper”  
  - You ratify, reject, or return  
  - You don’t edit conclave output  
  - Decision is separate, authoritative record  
  **Image description:** A judge’s gavel labeled “Keeper,” beside two documents: “Transcript” and “Decision.”  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - The transcript is evidence.  
  - The verdict is a recommendation.  
  - Your decision is the binding artifact.  
  - That separation keeps the audit trail clean.

<hr>

  - Run the fake adapter smoke test  
  - Confirm worker, database, orchestrator end-to-end

  - Start with a real conclave task  
  **Image description:** A “system checklist” with green checks: Orchestrator, Worker, DB, Dashboard.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - Before burning real quota, test the plumbing.

- ### Slide 13 — The Dashboard: Where Decisions Become Memory
  - Inbox filters by status, mode, export  
  - Detail view shows live activity  
  - Transcript and final result are first-class  
  - Decision panel turns work into record  
  **Image description:** A clean UI mock: Inbox list on left, transcript and Decision panel on right.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - The dashboard is your deliberation cockpit.  
  - You watch the task run in real time.  
  - Then you record what you actually chose.  
  - That’s when it becomes reusable.

<hr>

- ### Slide 14 — Slash Commands: Stay Inside Your Coding Flow
  - /conclave asks the full council  
  - /consult gets a bounded critique  
  - /continue threads follow-ups with context  
  - /decide writes your binding outcome  
  **Image description:** A terminal window with four highlighted commands, connected to a local service icon.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - You shouldn’t context-switch to get governance.  
  - Submit from the CLI you’re already using.  
  - Then record decisions without leaving flow.  
  - The tool meets you where you work.

<hr>

- ### Slide 15 — Sandboxes and Attachments: Read-Only By Design
  - Per-task sandbox copy of project path  
  - Hard caps prevent runaway disk usage  
  - Text and PDFs inline into prompts  
  - Images pass natively to CLI seats  
  **Image description:** A sealed “snapshot folder” stamped “READ ONLY,” plus file icons: PDF, MD, PNG.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - Agents see a snapshot, not your live repo.  
  - That keeps deliberation grounded and stable.  
  - Attachments become direct context, not vague references.  
  - And images stay multimodal, not lossy OCR.

<hr>

- ### Slide 16 — Privacy: The Honest Claim
  - Orchestration state stays local  
  - Prompts still go to model providers  
  - OpenRouter data_collection defaults to deny  
  - Control of audit trail is the win  
  **Image description:** Two pipes: “Local state” staying inside a laptop; “Model prompts” flowing outward to provider clouds.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - Switchboard is not a magic privacy cloak.  
  - Your prompts still hit your providers.  
  - The promise is control of the orchestration plane.  
  - And durable, inspectable reasoning at home.

<hr>

- ### Slide 17 — Retention and Export: Keep What Matters
  - Tiered retention trims transcripts first  
  - Decisions and unresolved dissent never trimmed  
  - Export marks “preserved” for safety  
  - Bulk export snapshots unexported tasks  
  **Image description:** A three-tier shelf: Tier 1 locked, Tier 2 archived box, Tier 3 recyclable papers.  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - A real memory system must manage growth.  
  - Switchboard keeps the most important artifacts.  
  - It trims safely, with clear rules.  
  - And exports let you preserve deliberately.

<hr>

- ### Slide 18 — The Loop That Actually Works
  - Deliberate with the council  
  - Decide in your own words  
  - Execute in your interactive CLI  
  - Record the decision for future reuse  
  **Image description:** A four-step circular loop diagram: Deliberate → Decide → Execute → Record, ending at “Decision Library.”  
  **Speaker notes:**
  - This is the practical takeaway.  
  - Don’t let tools collapse steps into a blur.  
  - Deliberate, then decide, then execute, then record.  
  - That’s how you scale judgment, not just output.
